PixelRAG outperforms text parsers, reduces AI agent token costs by 10x

Researchers from UC Berkeley, Princeton, EPFL, and Databricks released PixelRAG, a framework that retrieves information from web pages using screenshots instead of text parsing, achieving up to 18.1% higher accuracy and reducing AI agent token costs by 10x. The key implication is that text conversion has been a major source of errors in retrieval-augmented generation systems, and treating documents as visual content preserves critical layout and structural information that traditional parsing destroys.

This threatens long-term AI development unless companies preserve access to authentic human-generated training data, creating a competitive advantage for AI vendors with proprietary data sources over those relying on public web content.

CloudCons: A Comprehensive End-to-End Benchmark for Cloud Resource Consolidation

28
/28

Xiaobin Zhang, Lefei Shen, Mouxiang Chen et al.

 
CloudCons builds a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating forecasting models in cloud resource consolidation. It integrates datasets from Huawei Cloud, Microsoft Azure, and Google Borg with diverse workload patterns, then tests statistical, deep learning, and foundation models through a forecast-then-optimize pipeline. The key finding: foundation models achieve superior prediction accuracy but don't necessarily improve actual consolidation decisions compared to simpler baselines.
This work matters because it exposes a critical gap between forecasting accuracy and real-world utility. Cloud operators can't assume better predictions automatically translate to better resource efficiency or reliability. The paper provides actionable guidance on selecting predictive quantiles to balance efficiency and reliability, offering practical insights for deployment that go beyond pure accuracy metrics.

EquiDexFlow: Contact-Grounded SE(3)-Equivariant Dexterous Grasp Generative Flows

27
/28

Clinton Enwerem, John S. Baras, Calin Belta

 
EquiDexFlow jointly generates robot hand configurations, finger contact positions, and contact forces from object point clouds in a single pass. The method uses SE(3)-equivariant neural networks (which preserve rotational symmetry) and flow matching on Lie groups to ensure that rotating an object produces proportionally rotated grasps without retraining. Critically, it projects predicted forces into the Coulomb friction cone by construction, guaranteeing friction compliance automatically rather than relying on loss penalties. Trained on 8,100 simulated force-closure grasps, the model achieves zero friction violations and proves end-to-end equivariance empirically with wrist residuals below 0.04 degrees.
This bridges a gap in dexterous manipulation: previous methods decouple pose prediction from force feasibility, generating kinematically plausible but physically infeasible grasps. EquiDexFlow's joint learning of geometry and physics enables it to rank and execute grasps reliably. The authors demonstrate this on a real 16-DoF Allegro Hand, successfully executing picks on test objects including at rotated poses. The work sets a foundation for embodiment-agnostic grasp generation where physical constraints are architecture guarantees, not optimization afterthoughts.

scLLM-DSC: LLM-Knowledge Enhanced Cross-Modal Deep Structural Clustering for Single-Cell RNA Sequencing

26
/28

Ping Xu, Pengjiang Li, Tian Du et al.

 
# scLLM-DSC: Bridging Biology and Deep Learning for Cell Clustering
Researchers combined large language models with deep learning to improve how scientists identify cell types in single-cell RNA sequencing data. The method, called scLLM-DSC, uses two complementary approaches: it encodes biological knowledge from gene databases as semantic representations and extracts topological structure from expression patterns. A cross-modal alignment mechanism then harmonizes these two views into a unified space, optimized specifically for clustering rather than general-purpose tasks. Benchmarks show significant improvements over eleven existing methods.
Current cell-clustering tools ignore what genes actually do biologically, treating them as abstract numbers. This framework treats gene knowledge as first-class information, enabling clustering outputs that map directly to biological cell types rather than abstract clusters. For practitioners analyzing complex tissues, this means faster, more interpretable discoveries. The approach generalizes across different language models and datasets, suggesting real practical utility beyond this single implementation.
